Happy Birthday, Sarah

Mary had a little lamb
All white was this lamb's fleece.
Uncle Matt on the other hand
Had Sarah for a niece

So this email goes to you this day.
And I ask myself "Is there a
Better way for me to say
'Happy Birthday, Sarah.' ?"

-- Matt 8/7/06
Hey, Sarah,

I know this is a day early but knowing me, I'll forget tomorrow. Anyway, you've undoubtedly heard from your mother what an exceptional and deeply moving poet I am. If not, I'm sure you'd be interested in some of the breath taking works of poetry perpetrated by me and several of your mother's other siblings, as preserved for all time and all mankind at www.sickingfamily.com/culture/culture.htm

What this is all leading up to is that the Poetry Muse struck me again and I felt the need to write you a birthday poem. Well, not a poem exactly, more of a ditty. Well not even a ditty, just some words that sort of rhyme and come close to having the meaning I intended. So anyway the Muse struck me, perhaps a bit too hard. This was what came out.

Mary had a little lamb
All white was this lamb's fleece.
Uncle Matt on the other hand
Had Sarah for a niece

So this email goes to you this day.
And I ask myself "Is there a
Better way for me to say
'Happy Birthday, Sarah.' ?"

Okay so I'm not Robert Frost.

Love

Uncle Matt

PS Actually, there's probably all kinds of deeper meaning and metaphor and allegory and allusion and imagery and symbolism in the poem. Generally, though, despite my obvious talent in writing, I am somewhat lacking in my ability to analyze poetry. So I don't really know what this poem means. I have some ideas, though:
Mary had a little lamb This is clearly an allusion to your mom having given birth to you 18 years ago. What a sweet little lamb Mary Regis bore!
Or else it's about some shepherd named Mary. I mean, who can really tell? That's the magic and mystery of poetry.
All white was this lamb's fleece This is what's called "foreshadowing". In other words the poet is looking forward in time to something that's going to happen. In this case the thing that is going to happen is the fourth line of the poem - the one that that ends with "niece". Knowing that this line was going to happen, the poet realized he needed the second line to end with "fleece" or "cease" or "geese" or "police" or "John Cleese" - something that rhymes with "niece". But those other words didn't make sense in the context of the lamb. (This is a deep insight into how the creative mind works.)
Uncle Matt on the other hand
Had Sarah for a niece 		This is the poet's clever way of making sure that the reader realizes that this poem is about Sarah. Not just any Sarah, but Sarah Neff - the only Sarah of whom Matt is an uncle. It is also the poet's subtle way of getting his own name into the poem. This is called self-aggrandizement.
So this email goes to you this day.
And I ask myself, "Is there a
Better way for me to say
'Happy Birthday, Sarah.' ?" 		I think this whole stanza is fraught with imagery and all those other things. Notice the masculine rhyme of "there a" with "Sarah". This is a symbol for the fact that the poet was masculine (I prefer to think of the poet as not just masculine, but macho and buff, too, but I don't think the symbolism quite reaches that far.)
Also note the "I ask myself". This is imagery of the highest order for self-introspection. The poet is looking deep into himself and is feeling very self-satisfied. This is mainly because he's just finished reading most of the poem by now, and really likes this particular work of art. Also he's self-satisfied because - doing some more of the foreshadowing I mentioned earlier - he realizes that the poem will indeed have a "Happy Birthday, Sarah" message in it which was the reason he wrote the poem in the first place.

As you can probably guess I scored very high (a C+) in my Poetry Appreciation class back when I was in school.
